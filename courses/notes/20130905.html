Quantifiers and Translation

Hockey referees play hockey iff they skate well
$\forall t \in U, \; (R(t) \rightarrow (H(t) \leftrightarrow S(t)))$

Not to be confused with
$\forall t \in U, \; (H(t) \wedge R(t)) \leftrightarrow S(t)$

Nested Quantifiers

  • For every SU student $y$, there is an SU professor $z$ such that $y$ likes $z$.
    $\forall y \in S, \; \exists z \in P, \; L(y,z)$
  • There is a professor that every student likes.
    $\exists z \in P, \; \forall y \in S, \; L(y,z)$
In general...
  • If you have different quantifiers, the order is very important.
    Example: $\forall x \in X, \; \exists y \in Y, \; D(x,y) \; \not \equiv \; \exists y \in Y, \; \forall x \in X \; D(x,y) $
  • If you have the same quantifier, the order does not matter.
    Example: $\forall x \in X, \; \forall y \in Y, \; D(x, y) \; \equiv \; \forall y \in Y, \; \forall x \in X \; D(x, y) $

Beware of Nested Existentials

Two nested existentials ($\exists$) selecting from the same set do not necessarily select two different elements.

For example, if $U = \{1,2,3\}$, the formula $\exists x, \, \exists y, \, D(x,y)$ might choose $x = 1$ and $y = 1$.

More complicated example:

$\exists x \in V, \; \exists y \in P, \exists z \in P, (C(x) \wedge O(y, x) \wedge O(z, x) \wedge y \neq z)$

There is a vehicle $x$, and two people $y$, $z$, such that that $x$ is a car, $y$ owns $x$, $z$ owns $x$, and $y$ and $z$ are different people.

Colloquially: there is a car owned by at least two people.

If we did not say $y \neq z$, we might inadvertently be talking about a car owned by one person named Tom because the quantifiers would select $y = \text{Tom}$ and $z = \text{Tom}$.
