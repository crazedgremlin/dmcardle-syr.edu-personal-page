Induction Summary

Mathematical Induction

Basis: $$P(b)$$ [where $b$ is the lowest value you want to prove $P$ for]

IS: \begin{align} P(k) & \rightarrow & P(k+1)\\ \text{assume} & & \text{show} \end{align}

Structural Induction

Basis: $$P(b)$$ [for every base element $b$]

IS: \[ P(T_1) \wedge P(T_2) \rightarrow P(T_1 \star T_2) \]

Countability

Countable: finite or same cardinality as $\mathbb{Z}^+$

Uncountable: Not countable

Some countable sets: $\mathbb{Z}^+$, $\mathbb{N}$, $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Z}\times \mathbb{Z}$

Some uncountable sets: $\{ r \in \mathbb{R} \mid 0 < r < 1 \}$, $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})$

Ways to show set $X$ is countable

  • Show $X$ is finite
  • Show there is a bijection between $X$ and any known countable set
  • Give an enumeration strategy for $X$ — basically giving an algorithm for listing all the elements in the set
  • Show $X$ is a subset of a known countable set
  • Show $X$ is the union or cartesian product of two countable sets

Ways to show set $X$ is uncountable

  • Show $X$ is infinite. Simple counterexample proving this won't work: $\mathbb{Z}$ is countable and infinite.
  • Give bijection between $X$ and a known uncountable set
  • Show that a known uncountable set is a subset of $X$. Be careful about the direction of the subset relationship!
  • Use a Cantor-style diagonalization argument tos how no enumeration of $X$ exists

Cantor-Style Diagonalization Proof

Claim: $\{ r \in \mathbb{R} \mid 0 < r < 1 \}$ is uncountable.

Proof: (by contradiction)
Suppose $W = \{ r \in \mathbb{R} \mid 0 < r < 1 \}$ is countable.
Because $W$ is countable, there is an enumeration of its elements \[ r_1 \; r_2 \; r_3 \; r_4 \; \ldots \]

Each element $r_i \in W$ can be represented as a sequence of digits: \begin{align} r_1 = 0. \; d_{1,1} \; d_{1,2} \; d_{1,3} \; d_{1,4} \; \ldots \\ r_2 = 0. \; d_{2,1} \; d_{2,2} \; d_{2,3} \; d_{2,4} \; \ldots \\ r_3 = 0. \; d_{3,1} \; d_{3,2} \; d_{3,3} \; d_{3,4} \; \ldots \\ \end{align} The $j$th digit of $r_i$ is $d_{i,j} \in \{ 0, 1, 2, \ldots, 9\}$

There is an element $w \in W$ defined by \[ w = 0. \; e_1 \; e_2 \; e_3 \; e_4 \ldots \] where \[e_i = \cases{ 4 & \text{ if } d_{i,i} \neq 4 \cr 5 & \text{ if } d_{i,i} = 4 }\]

We know $w \neq r_1$ because they differ in the first digit. We also know $w \neq r_2$ because they differ in the second digit. And so on... Thus, $w$ is not any of the $r_i$ that appear in the enumeration. This means $w \in W$ and $w \not \in W$. This is a contradiction!
