Principle of Mathematical Induction

(not to be confused with Philosophical Induction)

For extra reading on induction, see the first chapter of The Art of Computer Programming by Donald Knuth.

Let $P(w)$ be a predicate that depends on the integer variable $w$ and let $b$ be an integer.

Symbolically, mathematical induction is defined as follows: \begin{align} &P(b)\\ &\forall k \geq b, (P(k) \rightarrow P(k+1))\\ &\therefore \forall n \geq b, P(n) \end{align}

To show that $P(n)$ is true for all integers $n \geq b$, it suffices to do

  1. Basis Step: Verify $P(b)$ is true.
  2. Inductive Step: Show that for all $k \geq b$, the conditional $P(k) \rightarrow P(k+1)$ is true.

    In proving (2), we must suppose $P(k)$ is true. This is called our Induction Hypothesis.

Summation Notation is just shorthand notation for repeated addition, sort of like a for loop. \[ \sum_{i=0}^{n} i = 0 + 1 + 2 + 3 + \ldots + n \] More generally, \[ \sum_{j=i}^n j = i + (i+1) + (i+2) + \ldots + (n-2) + (n-1) + n \]

Claim:

For every positive integer $m$, \[ \sum_{j=1}^{m} j = \frac{m(m+1)}{2} \]

It is impossible to prove this through brute force because there are infinite positive integers. We need to use induction.

Informally:

Let $P(n)$ be the statement: \[ \sum_{j=1}^n j = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \]

  1. Show $P(1)$ is true
  2. Show that for all $k \geq 1$, $(P(k) \rightarrow P(k+1))$.

Proof: (by mathematical induction)

Basis:

$P(1)$ is true because \[\sum_{i=1}^{1} i = 1 = \frac{1(1+1)}{2}\]

Inductive Step:

Suppose $k \geq 1$. For the inductive hypothesis, suppose $P(k)$ is true: \[ \sum_{j=1}^k j = \frac{k (k+1}{2} \]

We need to show $P(k+1)$ is true (what follows is NOT YET known to be true): \[ \sum _{j=1} ^ {k+1} j = \frac{(k + 1) (k + 1 + 1)}{2} \]

We see that \begin{align} \sum_{j=1}^{k+1}j &= (\sum_{j=1}^k j) + (k+1) \\ &= \frac{k(k+1)}{2} + (k+1) \tag{by def of $\sum$} \\ &= \frac{k(k+1) + 2(k+1)}{2} \\ &= \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2} = \frac{(k+1)(k+1+1)}{2} \end{align}

Thus, by the basis, inductive step, and the principle of mathematical induction (PMI), the claim is true.
