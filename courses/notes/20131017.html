$f$unctions

Let $X$, $Y$ be sets. A function $f$ from $X$ to $Y$ (written $f: X \rightarrow Y$) is a relation from $X$ to $Y$ s.t. for each element $x\in X$, there is exactly one $y \in Y$ such that $(x,y)\in f$.

[We will often write $f(x)=y$ to indicate $(x,y)\in f$.]

"Function $f$ maps $x$ to $y$."

Definitions

Suppose $f : X \rightarrow Y$.

  • The domain of $f$ is $X$
  • The codomain of $f$ is $Y$
  • Suppose $f(a)=b$
    • $b$ is the image of $a$
    • $a$ is a preimage of b
  • The range of $f$ is the set of all images of elements of $X$.

Types of Functions

  • $f$ is injective (one-to-one, 1-1) if, for all $x_1, x_2 \in X,\\ (f(x_1) = f(x_2)) \rightarrow (x_1 = x_2)$.

    In other words, each domain element maps to a different codomain element. Consider the contrapositive of the definition given above: $(x_1 \neq x_2) \to (f(x_1) \neq f(x_2))$.

  • $f$ is surjective (onto) iff, for all $y \in Y,\\ \exists x \in X, \; f(x) = y$.

    In other words, every element in the codomain has something in the domain mapping to it.

    In more different words, $f$ is surjective iff its codomain and range are the same set.

  • $f$ is bijective iff it is both 1-1 and onto

  • Be careful!

    A 1-1 function is not the same thing as a 1-1 correspondence. A 1-1 correspondence means there is a bijection.

Operations on Functions

Suppose $f : X \to Y$ and $g : Y \to Z$.
  • The composition of $f$ and $g$ is $$g \circ f : X \to Z$$ defined by $$(g \circ f) a = g ( f ( a ) )$$

    Associativity of composition: $f_7 \circ (f_6 \circ f_5) = (f_7 \circ f_6) \circ f_5$

  • Two functions $f,g: X \to Y$ are equal ($f = g$) iff $\forall a \in X, f(a) = g(a)$

Theorem: Let $f:X\to Y$, $g : Y \to Z$, $h : Z \to W$ be functions. Then $h \circ (g \circ f) = (h \circ g ) \circ f$.

Proof (direct): [NTS: for all $a \in X$, $h \circ (g \circ f) = (h \circ g ) \circ f$] Consider arbitrary $a \in X$. We see by the definition of $\circ$ that $$ \begin{align} (h \circ (g \circ f)) (a) &= h( (g \circ f) a)\\ &= h(g(f(a))) \\ &= (h \circ g) (f(a)) \\ &= ((h \circ g) \circ f) (a) \end{align} $$ Because $a$ was arbitrary, the theorem is true.
