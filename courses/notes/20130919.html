Quantifier Review

To make $\exists y \in U, P(y)$ true, you only need a single $a \in U$ such that $P(a)$ is true.
To make $\exists y \in U, P(y)$ false, you must show every element makes $P(a)$ false.

Example: show $\exists y \in U, (Q(y)\rightarrow R(y))$ is false and $U= \{\text{red}, \text{blue}, \text{green} \}$.
You must show $$ \neg \left[Q(\text{red}) \rightarrow R(\text{red}) \right] \wedge \neg \left[Q(\text{blue}) \rightarrow R(\text{blue})\right] \wedge \neg \left[Q(\text{green}) \rightarrow R(\text{green})\right] $$
Therefore, $\forall y \in U, \neg \left( Q(y) \rightarrow R(y) \right)$.

To make $\forall y \in U, P(y)$ is true, you need to show that every $w \in U$ makes $P(w)$ true.
To make $\forall y \in U, P(y)$ is false, you need to show that a single $w \in U$ makes $P(w)$ false.
Universal Negation: $\neg \left( \forall y \in U, P(y) \right) \equiv \exists y \in U, \neg P(y)$
Existential Negation: $\neg \left( \exists y \in U, P(y) \right) \equiv \forall y \in U, \neg P(y)$

Writing Math-Style Proofs

Symbolic logic provides a backdrop for understanding how mathematicians prove things, as well as their lingo
  • Definition: a statement that stipulates the meaning of a new term, symbol, or object. A definition specifies precisely what is meant by the term and serves as the sole authority of what the term means.
    Example:

    Definition: An integer $n$ is even if there exists an integer $k$ such that $n = 2k$.

    [Object] x is [defined term] if [defining property about x].

  • Postulate (also called "axiom") is a statement assumed to be true, without proof. Serve as starting points from which other statements can be derived.
  • Theorem: statement that follows logically from axioms or other statements that have already been established. To be called a theorem, a statement must have a proof. A lemma is a theorem used to prove another theorem.
  • Claim: statement that we intend to prove.
  • Conjecture: a statement that is thought to be true but has not been proved
  • A counterexample to a statement is a value that shows the statement to be false.

Mathematical Proofs

  • The results tend to be expressed in paragraphs
  • May not spell out all the details (depends on audience)
  • Variables tend to be inplicitly universally quantified, unless specified otherwise
Every proof has certain features
  • A collection of hypotheses (or premises) $H_1, H_2, \ldots, H_k$
  • desired conclusion $C$
  • need to show that hypotheses when taken together logically imply conclusion: $$(H_1 \wedge H_2 \wedge \ldots \wedge H_k) \rightarrow C$$
For the time being, we'll stick to direct proofs.

Format for Proofs

  1. Explicitly specify what you're trying to prove
    Claim: For all integers $n$, if $n$ is even, then $n+1$ is odd.
  2. Label start of proof and indicate method
    Proof: (direct)
  3. Explicitly state any initial assumptions / constraints
    Consider an arbitrary integer $m$ such that $m$ is even
  4. Explicitly state what you need to show, unwrapping the stopping condition as necessary
    Need to show: $m+1$ is odd. That is, there exists an integer $k$ such that $m+1 = 2k+1$
  5. Fill in the gaps between steps 3 and 4 with the heart of the proof
  6. Explicitly wrap up the proof
    Because $m$ was arbitrary, the claim is true
